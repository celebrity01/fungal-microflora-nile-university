pathogens underscores the need for improved hygiene and ventilation.

assessment.

INTRODUCTION

1.1 Background of the Study.

Bacteria are single-celled microorganisms that are found in almost every
habitat on earth. They are considered one of the earliest life forms on
the planet and are found in various shapes and sizes. Bacteria can be
classified into three main shapes -- spherical (cocci), rod-shaped
(bacilli), or spiral (spirilla). Some bacteria can also form colonies or
chains. While some bacteria are beneficial and play vital roles in the
environment and human health, others can be harmful and cause diseases
(WHO 2017). Harmful bacteria are responsible for various infections such
as pneumonia, urinary tract infections, food poisoning, and skin
infections. Bacteria can be found in various environments such as soil,
water, air, and even inside the human body (WHO 2017). They have diverse
metabolic capabilities, allowing them to obtain energy from various
sources, including sunlight, organic matter, and even chemicals. Some
bacteria are capable of photosynthesis, while others rely on organic
matter or inorganic compounds for their energy needs. Bacteria reproduce
mostly through a process called binary fission, where a single cell
divides into two daughter cells. This allows them to multiply rapidly
under favorable conditions.

Science laboratories are controlled environments designed for research,
experimentation, and teaching across various scientific disciplines.
Despite their intended use, these laboratories often harbor a diverse
range of microorganisms, some of which may include opportunistic or
pathogenic bacteria. These bacteria, collectively referred to as
microflora, can accumulate on surfaces, equipment, ventilation systems,
and consumables, posing risks to the health of laboratory users and the
integrity of experimental processes (Chng et al., 2020).

The presence of bacterial contamination in laboratories may result from
multiple factors, including human activities, inadequate cleaning
protocols, environmental conditions, and contaminated biological
samples. Laboratories are typically designed to maintain a sterile or
semi-sterile environment, but deviations from standard safety protocols
can lead to the proliferation of microorganisms. Factors such as high
humidity, poor ventilation, and irregular sanitation practices can
exacerbate microbial growth (Kumar et al., 2023).

At Nile University of Nigeria, the UBANGI Complex hosts a range of
laboratories supporting disciplines like microbiology, chemistry,
biochemistry, and molecular biology. As a multidisciplinary facility, it
handles diverse activities that increase the likelihood of bacterial
cross-contamination. The bacteriological quality of these laboratories
is paramount, as it directly affects users\' safety, teaching
efficiency, and research outcomes. Microbial contamination could lead to
potential health hazards, including skin infections, respiratory issues,
and other illnesses, particularly in immune compromised individuals.

The presence of unwanted microflora in laboratories may interfere with
scientific experiments, leading to erroneous results or failed
experiments (Chng et al., 2020). This is particularly critical in
microbiology and molecular biology laboratories, where precision and
accuracy are required for the analysis of microbial populations and
their behaviors (Kumar et al., 2023). Understanding the bacterial load
and diversity within these laboratories is essential for developing
appropriate mitigation strategies.

LITERATURE REVIEW

2.1 Microflora

Microflora refers to the diverse community of microorganisms, including
bacteria, fungi, archaea, and viruses, that inhabit various environments
such as soil, water, air, the human body, and laboratory settings. These
microbial populations play crucial roles in ecological balance,
biological processes, and industrial applications. In natural
ecosystems, microflora contributes to nutrient cycling, organic matter
decomposition, and symbiotic relationships with plants and animals (Jang
et al., 2021). Soil microflora, including nitrogen-fixing bacteria like
Rhizobium, enhances soil fertility by converting atmospheric nitrogen
into a bioavailable form for plants. Similarly, gut microflora in
humans, composed of beneficial bacteria such as Lactobacillus and
Bifidobacterium, aid digestion, boost immunity, and protect against
pathogenic infections. However, microflora can also include
opportunistic pathogens like Escherichia coli and Staphylococcus aureus,
which may cause infections under favourable conditions. The composition
of microflora is dynamic and influenced by factors such as environmental
conditions, nutrient availability, temperature, and pH, making it a
critical area of study in microbiology and biotechnology (Serratore et
al., 2019; Zhou & Li, 2021).

The significance of microflora extends to various applied sciences,
including medicine, food production, agriculture, and biotechnology. In
medical research, understanding microflora is essential for developing
antibiotics, probiotics, and vaccines to combat infectious diseases. The
emergence of antibiotic-resistant bacterial strains, such as
Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) and
multidrug-resistant Pseudomonas aeruginosa, has heightened the need for
studying microbial genetics and resistance mechanisms. In food and dairy
industries, microbial fermentation processes driven by lactic acid
bacteria (Lactococcus and Streptococcus) are used to produce cheese,
yogurt, and probiotics. Additionally, industrial microbiology harnesses
microflora for biofuel production, bioremediation, and pharmaceutical
synthesis. Despite their beneficial roles, unwanted microbial
contamination in laboratories, hospitals, and food processing units
remains a challenge, necessitating stringent microbial control measures.
Advanced techniques such as metagenomics, next-generation sequencing,
and real-time PCR have revolutionized the study of microflora, enabling
precise identification and monitoring of microbial communities across
different environments. Understanding and managing microflora is crucial
for improving health, sustaining ecosystems, and advancing
biotechnological innovations (Ranjha et al., 2022).

2.2 Overview of Microflora in Science Laboratories

Microflora in science laboratories consists of a diverse range of
microorganisms, including bacteria, fungi, and yeasts, which can either
be naturally present or introduced through human activity, equipment,
and environmental factors. These microorganisms can have both beneficial
and detrimental effects on laboratory research, influencing experimental
accuracy, contamination risks, and biosafety concerns. Common laboratory
microflora includes Staphylococcus aureus, Escherichia coli, Pseudomonas
aeruginosa, Bacillus spp., and fungal species like Aspergillus and
Penicillium, many of which can be isolated from work surfaces,
instruments, and air samples. Their presence is influenced by factors
such as temperature, humidity, ventilation, and nutrient availability,
making laboratory environments susceptible to microbial colonization and
biofilm formation on surfaces and equipment. While some laboratory
microflora are essential for scientific research, such as probiotic
bacteria used in microbiological studies, opportunistic and pathogenic
strains can lead to cross-contamination, inaccurate results, and
compromised sterility. Antibiotic-resistant bacteria, including
Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) and
multidrug-resistant Klebsiella pneumoniae, pose additional risks,
necessitating strict biosafety protocols. To mitigate these challenges,
laboratories implement rigorous contamination control measures such as
autoclaving, chemical disinfection, high-efficiency particulate air
(HEPA) filtration, and adherence to Good Laboratory Practices (GLP).
Additionally, advanced microbial assessment techniques, including
PCR-based detection and next-generation sequencing, are used to monitor
and manage laboratory microflora effectively. By maintaining strict
microbial control and biosafety standards, science laboratories can
ensure the integrity of research, prevent contamination-related
setbacks, and safeguard the health of laboratory personnel (Maraz &
Khan, 2021; Matthews et al., 2025).

2.3 Types of Microflora Commonly Found in Laboratory Environments

Science laboratories harbour a wide variety of microflora, including
bacteria, fungi, and viruses, which can be introduced through human
activity, air, contaminated equipment, or experimental samples. Among
bacterial species, Staphylococcus aureus, Escherichia coli, Pseudomonas
aeruginosa, and Bacillus spp. are frequently encountered on laboratory
surfaces, workbenches, and equipment. Staphylococcus aureus, a common
skin microbe, can be introduced by lab personnel and may persist on
surfaces, leading to potential cross-contamination of biological
samples. Escherichia coli, typically associated with faecali
contamination, is often found in microbiology and biomedical research
laboratories, where improper handling of bacterial cultures can result
in its unintentional spread. Pseudomonas aeruginosa, an opportunistic
pathogen, thrives in moist environments such as water baths and
incubators, while Bacillus spp. are spore-forming bacteria capable of
surviving harsh conditions, making them particularly resilient
contaminants in laboratory settings. Additionally, lactic acid bacteria
like Lactobacillus and Lactococcus are commonly used in fermentation and
probiotic research, contributing beneficially to laboratory studies
while also presenting a risk of cross-contamination in sensitive
experiments (Makhoahle & Gaseitsiwe, 2022).

Fungi are another significant component of laboratory microflora, with
species such as Aspergillus, Penicillium, and Candida frequently found
in these environments. Aspergillus and Penicillium are airborne fungal
spores that can settle on laboratory surfaces, particularly in areas
with high humidity and poor ventilation. These fungi pose a risk to both
experimental integrity and human health, as they can produce allergens,
mycotoxins, and opportunistic infections in immunocompromised
individuals. Candida spp., including Candida albicans, are yeast-like
fungi that can contaminate laboratory cultures and medical research
samples, especially in microbiology and clinical laboratories. Fungal
contamination is particularly concerning in cleanroom environments and
biosafety laboratories, where maintaining sterility is critical for
research accuracy and safety. Improper ventilation, inadequate
decontamination, and high-moisture conditions favour fungal
proliferation, necessitating strict environmental control measures such
as HEPA filtration and routine sterilization protocols to minimize
fungal contamination risks (Sham et al., 2021).

Viruses, though less frequently discussed in the context of laboratory
microflora, can also be present, particularly in biomedical and virology
research facilities. Airborne viruses, such as Influenza viruses and
respiratory syncytial virus (RSV), can be introduced by infected lab
personnel and persist in laboratory environments if proper biosafety
measures are not followed. Laboratory-acquired infections (LAIs) from
viruses such as Hepatitis B virus (HBV), Hepatitis C virus (HCV), and
SARS-CoV-2 have been documented in clinical research settings where
biological specimens, including blood and tissue cultures, are handled.
Viral contamination poses a significant risk to laboratory personnel and
experimental samples, making the use of biosafety cabinets, personal
protective equipment (PPE), and proper decontamination protocols
essential. Additionally, viral contamination in cell culture
laboratories can lead to erroneous research results, as latent viruses
in contaminated cell lines can interfere with genetic and biochemical
assays (Vandenberg et al., 2020; Cornish et al., 2021).

2.4 Sources of Microbial Contamination in Laboratories

Microbial contamination in laboratories originates from multiple
sources, including human activity, laboratory equipment, air, water, and
contaminated reagents or samples. Lab personnel are a major contributor,
as microbes from their skin, clothing, and respiratory secretions can
transfer onto surfaces and instruments.

2.4.1 Human Activity as a Source of Microbial Contamination in
Laboratories

Human activity plays a crucial role in the introduction and spread of
microflora in science laboratories. Lab personnel, through routine
movements and interactions with equipment, unintentionally transfer
microorganisms from their skin, clothing, and respiratory secretions
onto surfaces and experimental setups. Studies have shown that
human-associated bacteria, such as Staphylococcus aureus, Micrococcus
spp., and Corynebacterium spp., are frequently isolated from laboratory
benches, door handles, and shared equipment. These microorganisms can
compromise the sterility of laboratory environments, potentially
affecting research outcomes and posing health risks to researchers
(Machado‐Moreira et al., 2019).

Clothing worn by lab personnel serves as a significant vehicle for
microbial contamination. Regular attire, including lab coats, gloves,
and masks, can harbour and transmit bacteria and fungi if not properly
managed. For example, research has indicated that lab coats frequently
become contaminated with bacteria such as Escherichia coli and Bacillus
spp., particularly in microbiology and biomedical laboratories. If these
garments are not regularly washed or replaced, they can act as
reservoirs for microbes, increasing the risk of cross-contamination
between different laboratory areas. The use of dedicated lab clothing
and proper laundering practices is essential to minimizing microbial
spread (Weyrich et al., 2019).

Hygiene practices among laboratory personnel are another critical factor
influencing microbial contamination. Poor hand hygiene has been
identified as a major contributor to the transfer of pathogenic bacteria
in laboratory settings. Hands can carry a variety of microbes, including
antibiotic-resistant strains such as Methicillin-resistant
Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA), which can persist on surfaces and
contaminate samples. Proper handwashing techniques, along with the use
of alcohol-based hand sanitizers, have been proven effective in reducing
microbial load and preventing contamination. Implementing strict hand
hygiene protocols is vital in maintaining a controlled laboratory
environment (Cavicchioli et al., 2019).

Respiratory secretions also contribute to microbial contamination in
laboratories, particularly in environments where airborne transmission
is a concern. Talking, sneezing, or coughing can release aerosols
containing bacteria and viruses, which can settle on work surfaces,
cultures, and laboratory instruments. Pathogens such as Mycobacterium
tuberculosis and Influenza viruses can remain viable in the air and on
surfaces for extended periods, posing risks to both experiments and
laboratory personnel. The use of face masks and proper ventilation
systems can help mitigate the spread of airborne microbes, ensuring a
safer working environment (Wang et al., 2021).

Laboratory workflows and movement patterns significantly influence the
distribution of microflora. Frequent movement between different lab
sections, handling of multiple samples, and improper glove usage can
facilitate the spread of contaminants. For instance, researchers who
handle microbial cultures without changing gloves between tasks may
unknowingly transfer bacteria from one sample to another, leading to
inaccurate results. Establishing clear protocols for workflow
management, such as designated areas for handling sterile materials and
regular glove changes, can reduce the risk of cross-contamination (Zhu
et al., 2020).

Human activity is a major contributor to microbial contamination in
science laboratories. Lab personnel introduce and spread microbes
through direct contact, clothing, hygiene practices, respiratory
emissions, and movement patterns. Implementing strict hygiene protocols,
wearing appropriate lab attire, and adhering to best practices for
laboratory conduct are essential in minimizing microbial contamination.
By addressing these human-related factors, laboratories can maintain a
sterile environment, ensuring the integrity of scientific research and
the safety of laboratory personnel (Abatenh et al., 2018).

2.4.2​ Laboratory equipment and surfaces

Laboratory equipment and surfaces serve as major reservoirs for
microbial contamination, significantly influencing the sterility and
reliability of experimental results. Frequently used instruments such as
pipettes, centrifuges, microscopes, and biosafety cabinets can
accumulate microbial populations if not properly sanitized. Studies have
shown that bacteria such as Staphylococcus aureus, Escherichia coli, and
Pseudomonas aeruginosa are commonly isolated from laboratory surfaces,
especially in areas with high human contact (Chaoui et al., 2019). These
microorganisms can persist on surfaces for extended periods, creating
potential cross-contamination risks. Additionally, biofilm formation on
frequently used equipment, such as water baths and incubators, can
provide a protective environment for bacteria and fungi, making them
more resistant to standard cleaning procedures. Regular decontamination
using disinfectants, UV sterilization, and proper maintenance protocols
are crucial in minimizing microbial load on laboratory equipment (Jones
et al., 2020).

Surfaces within laboratory environments, including workbenches, floors,
and fume hoods, are also prone to microbial accumulation due to frequent
exposure to biological samples, human activity, and environmental
factors. Porous and textured surfaces can harbour bacteria and fungi,
allowing them to persist despite routine cleaning efforts. Research has
highlighted that laboratory surfaces in microbiology and biomedical
research facilities often contain antibiotic-resistant bacteria,
emphasizing the need for stringent decontamination practices.
Implementing surface-specific cleaning agents, maintaining a structured
cleaning schedule, and using antimicrobial coatings on high-touch
surfaces can significantly reduce microbial presence. By enforcing
strict hygiene protocols and regular equipment sterilization,
laboratories can enhance safety, ensure experimental accuracy, and
prevent contamination-related research setbacks (Sayers, 2021; Seseña et
al., 2022).

2.4.3​ Airborne microorganisms in Laboratory Environments

Airborne microorganisms pose a significant challenge in maintaining
sterility and safety in laboratory environments. These microorganisms,
including bacteria, fungi, and viruses, can be suspended in the air for
extended periods and settle on surfaces, equipment, and experimental
samples. Common airborne microbes found in laboratories include
Staphylococcus aureus, Bacillus spp., Aspergillus spp., and Cladosporium
spp., which originate from human activity, ventilation systems, and
external environmental sources. Airborne contamination can compromise
experimental accuracy, particularly in microbiological and biomedical
research, where even trace amounts of unwanted microbes can alter
results. Poor air circulation, inadequate filtration, and high foot
traffic contribute to the persistence and spread of these
microorganisms, making air quality control a crucial aspect of
laboratory management (Gonzalez-Martin, 2019).

To mitigate the risks associated with airborne microorganisms,
laboratories implement various control measures such as high-efficiency
particulate air (HEPA) filtration, laminar airflow systems, and proper
ventilation. Biosafety cabinets and cleanrooms are designed to minimize
microbial exposure, especially in research involving pathogenic
organisms or sterile cultures. Additionally, regular air quality
monitoring using settle plates and air samplers helps detect microbial
presence and assess contamination risks. Personal protective equipment
(PPE), such as face masks, also plays a role in reducing airborne
microbial transmission from lab personnel. By maintaining strict air
control measures, laboratories can prevent airborne contamination,
ensuring both research integrity and the safety of personnel working in
these environments (Idris et al., 2020).

2.4.4 Contaminated reagents and samples as Sources of Microbial
Contamination

Contaminated reagents and samples are significant contributors to
microbial contamination in laboratory settings, potentially compromising
the accuracy and reliability of experimental results. Reagents,
including buffers, culture media, and chemical solutions, can become
contaminated during preparation, storage, or handling. Microbial
contamination in reagents often arises from improper sterilization
techniques, exposure to unfiltered air, or the use of contaminated water
sources. For instance, bacterial and fungal spores can survive in
inadequately sterilized solutions, leading to unwanted microbial growth
in cell cultures and biochemical assays. Such contamination not only
affects the reproducibility of experiments but can also lead to
misleading conclusions, especially in microbiological and biomedical
research. To prevent this, laboratories must enforce stringent quality
control measures, including regular sterility testing of reagents,
proper storage conditions, and adherence to aseptic techniques during
preparation and use (Gerba & Pepper, 2019).

Similarly, biological samples used in research, such as blood, tissue,
water, and environmental swabs, are highly susceptible to microbial
contamination. This can occur during collection, transport, or
processing if proper aseptic techniques are not followed.
Cross-contamination between samples is a major concern, as it can lead
to inaccurate microbial identification, misinterpretation of results,
and even the propagation of antibiotic-resistant strains. For example,
in molecular biology studies, the presence of DNA from unintended
microbial sources can interfere with PCR amplification, leading to
false-positive results. To mitigate these risks, laboratories must
implement strict sample handling protocols, use sterile collection
tools, and routinely validate sample integrity before experimentation.
By maintaining rigorous sterility standards for reagents and samples,
laboratories can ensure the validity of their research while minimizing
the risks associated with microbial contamination (Weyrich et al.,
2019).

2.5 Factors Influencing Microbial Growth in Laboratory Environments

Several factors influence microbial growth in laboratory environments,
affecting both beneficial and contaminant microflora. Temperature and
humidity, Nutrient availability, ventilation and air filtration systems,
improper hygiene practices, unsterilized equipment handling, and
frequent movement.

2.5.1​ Influence of Temperature and Humidity on Microbial Growth in
Laboratories

Temperature and humidity are two critical environmental factors that
significantly impact the survival, proliferation, and distribution of
microflora in science laboratories. Many bacteria, fungi, and other
microorganisms thrive within specific temperature ranges, with most
mesophilic bacteria, such as Escherichia coli and Staphylococcus aureus,
growing optimally between 20°C and 40°C. If laboratory temperatures fall
outside this range, microbial growth may slow down or cease, while
extreme heat can denature microbial proteins, leading to cell death.
Psychrophilic bacteria, such as Pseudomonas spp., can persist in cold
environments, posing contamination risks in laboratories storing
biological samples at refrigeration temperatures (Cruz-Paredes et al.,
2021). In addition to temperature, humidity plays a vital role in
microbial viability and transmission. High humidity levels (above 60%)
create favourable conditions for fungal growth, particularly for species
like Aspergillus and Penicillium, which can contaminate air, surfaces,
and even experimental cultures. On the other hand, low humidity (below
30%) can desiccate bacteria and viruses, but it may also promote the
formation of bioaerosols, increasing the risk of airborne microbial
dissemination. Improper control of temperature and humidity in
laboratory settings can thus lead to contamination of workspaces,
degradation of sensitive reagents, and compromised research integrity.
Therefore, maintaining optimal environmental conditions through climate
control systems, dehumidifiers, and air filtration units is essential to
prevent unwanted microbial proliferation and ensure laboratory sterility
(Dabisch et al., 2021).

2.5.2 ​ Nutrient Availability and Microbial Growth in Laboratory
Environments

Nutrient availability plays a fundamental role in determining the
composition and proliferation of microflora within science laboratories.
Microorganisms require specific nutrients, including carbon, nitrogen,
phosphorus, and trace elements, to grow and sustain metabolic
activities. In laboratory environments, these nutrients can be
inadvertently provided through various sources, such as biological
samples, culture media, residues from experiments, and organic matter
present on surfaces. For instance, improperly cleaned workbenches and
laboratory equipment can accumulate organic residues that serve as
substrates for microbial growth, fostering the development of biofilms
that are resistant to standard disinfection procedures. Additionally,
nutrient-rich environments such as incubators, autoclaves, and storage
areas for microbiological cultures can become hotspots for microbial
colonization if not properly maintained. The presence of moisture
further enhances the availability of nutrients by dissolving organic
compounds, making them more accessible to bacteria and fungi.
Opportunistic pathogens like Pseudomonas aeruginosa thrive in
nutrient-rich and moist conditions, posing a contamination risk in
laboratory settings. Moreover, airborne dust particles can carry organic
material that supports microbial survival, especially in areas with poor
ventilation. To mitigate the risks associated with nutrient
availability, laboratories must enforce stringent cleaning protocols,
regularly sterilize work surfaces, and properly dispose of organic
waste. Effective control of nutrient sources through proper waste
management, decontamination procedures, and restricted access to
sensitive areas can significantly reduce microbial contamination,
ensuring a sterile environment that supports reliable and reproducible
scientific research (Mills et al., 2019).

2.5.3 ​ Surface materials and biofilm formation

Surface materials in laboratory environments play a crucial role in
microbial contamination and biofilm formation, significantly impacting
laboratory hygiene and research integrity (Costa et al., 2019).
Different materials, such as stainless steel, glass, plastic, and
ceramic, exhibit varying degrees of microbial adherence and biofilm
development, influencing the persistence of bacteria and fungi on
laboratory surfaces. Biofilms, which are structured microbial
communities encased in a self-produced extracellular polymeric substance
(EPS), can form on virtually any surface, making contamination control
challenging. Studies have shown that rough, porous, and hydrophilic
materials, such as uncoated plastics and wooden surfaces, promote higher
microbial adhesion compared to smooth, non-porous materials like
polished stainless steel or glass. Once bacteria attach to a surface,
they undergo a series of developmental stages, leading to the formation
of mature biofilms that provide protection against environmental
stressors, including disinfectants and antibiotics. Common laboratory
biofilm-forming microorganisms include Pseudomonas aeruginosa,
Staphylococcus aureus, and Escherichia coli, which can persist on
surfaces for extended periods, posing significant risks to laboratory
sterility and experimental reliability. The presence of biofilms can
lead to contamination of research samples, erroneous experimental
results, and potential health hazards for laboratory personnel.
Effective biofilm management strategies involve the use of antimicrobial
surface coatings, rigorous cleaning protocols, and the implementation of
enzymatic or chemical treatments specifically designed to disrupt
biofilm matrices. Additionally, routine monitoring of laboratory
surfaces using ATP bioluminescence assays and microbial culture
techniques can help detect and prevent biofilm-associated contamination
(Carrascosa et al., 2021).

2.5.4​Ventilation and air filtration systems

Ventilation and air filtration systems play a critical role in
maintaining a controlled microbial environment within science
laboratories by regulating airflow, reducing airborne contaminants, and
ensuring air quality. Proper ventilation helps prevent the accumulation
of microbial aerosols, which can originate from human activity,
biological samples, and laboratory processes such as culturing bacteria
and fungi. Without adequate airflow, microorganisms such as
Staphylococcus aureus, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, and fungal spores like
Aspergillus can remain suspended in the air for extended periods,
increasing the risk of contamination. High-efficiency particulate air
(HEPA) filters are commonly used in laboratory ventilation systems to
capture airborne particles as small as 0.3 microns with 99.97%
efficiency, effectively removing bacteria, viruses, and fungal spores
from circulating air. In biosafety laboratories, laminar airflow systems
further enhance contamination control by directing sterile air over work
surfaces and preventing unfiltered air from entering critical zones.
Additionally, negative pressure ventilation is employed in
high-containment labs to ensure that hazardous airborne pathogens do not
escape into adjacent areas, thereby protecting both personnel and the
external environment. Regular maintenance of air filtration systems,
including timely replacement of filters and monitoring of airflow
efficiency, is essential to sustaining optimal laboratory conditions.
Failure to maintain proper ventilation can lead to increased microbial
loads, compromised research integrity, and heightened health risks for
laboratory personnel (Elsaid & Ahmed, 2021; Kraakman, 2021)

2.6​ Antibiotic Resistance and Biosecurity Concerns in Laboratory
Microflora

The emergence of antibiotic-resistant bacteria in laboratory
environments is a growing concern, as these microorganisms can
compromise research integrity and pose serious health risks. Antibiotic
resistance occurs when bacteria evolve mechanisms to survive exposure to
antimicrobial agents, rendering standard treatments ineffective.
Laboratories that handle bacterial cultures, particularly those involved
in clinical and microbiological research, can inadvertently contribute
to the spread of resistant strains. Common antibiotic-resistant bacteria
found in laboratory settings include Methicillin-resistant
Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA), Vancomycin-resistant Enterococci (VRE),
and multidrug-resistant Pseudomonas aeruginosa. These organisms can
persist on laboratory surfaces, equipment, and even within biofilms,
making their eradication difficult and increasing the risk of
cross-contamination (Davies & Wales, 2019; Muteeb et al., 2023).

Improper handling and disposal of bacterial cultures containing
resistant strains is one of the primary sources of antibiotic resistance
in laboratories. When antibiotic-resistant bacteria are not adequately
contained or decontaminated, they can spread to laboratory personnel,
equipment, and the surrounding environment. The misuse of antibiotics in
research settings, such as sub-lethal dosing or prolonged exposure, can
select for resistant populations, further exacerbating the problem. The
transfer of resistance genes between bacteria via horizontal gene
transfer (HGT) mechanisms, such as conjugation, transformation, and
transduction, allows resistance traits to spread rapidly within
laboratory microflora. This genetic exchange can result in the emergence
of highly resistant bacterial strains that are difficult to control
(Islam, 2021; Endale et al., 2023).

Biosecurity concerns arise when antibiotic-resistant laboratory
microflora escape containment and enter the general environment or
healthcare settings. Accidental release of resistant bacteria through
contaminated waste, laboratory effluents, or improper disposal of
biological materials can have significant public health implications.
For example, wastewater from research laboratories has been found to
contain antibiotic-resistant Escherichia coli and Klebsiella pneumoniae,
which can contribute to the spread of resistance in natural water
systems. If such bacteria enter clinical settings, they can compromise
the treatment of infections, leading to prolonged illnesses, increased
healthcare costs, and higher mortality rates. Proper biosafety measures,
including autoclaving biological waste, implementing strict waste
management protocols, and monitoring effluent discharge, are essential
in preventing the unintended release of resistant microorganisms (Li et
al., 2021).

The occupational risks associated with antibiotic-resistant laboratory
microflora highlight the need for stringent biosafety protocols.
Laboratory personnel working with resistant strains are at risk of
accidental exposure through inhalation, ingestion, or skin contact.
Studies have shown that researchers handling antibiotic-resistant
bacteria can carry these organisms on their skin or clothing,
potentially spreading them beyond the laboratory environment. To
mitigate these risks, laboratories must enforce biosafety level (BSL)
guidelines appropriate to the microorganisms being handled. This
includes using biosafety cabinets, wearing personal protective equipment
(PPE) such as gloves and masks, and following proper decontamination
procedures after handling bacterial cultures. Additionally, routine
screening of laboratory personnel for colonization by resistant bacteria
can help identify and control potential outbreaks (Macher et al., 2019;
Khan et al., 2019).

2.7 ​Impact of Microflora on Experimental Accuracy and Reliability

Microflora present in laboratory environments can significantly affect
the accuracy and reliability of scientific experiments, particularly in
microbiology, molecular biology, and biochemical research. Contaminant
microorganisms can interfere with experimental results by introducing
unwanted variables that alter the expected outcomes. For instance,
bacterial and fungal contamination in cell culture experiments can lead
to competition for nutrients, secretion of metabolic byproducts, and
alterations in cellular behaviour, ultimately compromising data
integrity. Studies have shown that airborne bacteria such as
Staphylococcus spp. and Bacillus spp. frequently contaminate culture
media, leading to erroneous conclusions and failed experiments.
Additionally, in molecular biology, the presence of microbial DNA can
result in false-positive or misleading PCR results, affecting genetic
analyses and diagnostic accuracy. Such contamination not only wastes
resources and time but also undermines the reproducibility of scientific
findings, which is a cornerstone of credible research (Van Niekerk,
2022).

Beyond direct contamination of samples, microflora can also affect the
performance of laboratory equipment and reagents, further reducing
experimental reliability. Biofilm formation on surfaces, pipettes, and
water purification systems can lead to persistent microbial presence
that alters reagent composition and experiment conditions. For example,
the presence of Pseudomonas aeruginosa in laboratory water systems has
been documented to interfere with biochemical assays by introducing
extracellular enzymes that degrade assay components. Similarly,
microbial growth in storage solutions and reagents can lead to chemical
degradation, reducing their efficacy and producing inconsistent results.
The impact of such contamination is particularly critical in clinical
and pharmaceutical research, where microbial interference can affect
drug formulation, sterility testing, and quality control processes
(Ahmed et al., 2022; Osaghae et al., 2024)

2.8 Implications of Microflora Assessment for Laboratory Safety and
Research Integrity

The assessment of microflora in scientific laboratory complexes carries
significant implications for both laboratory safety and research
integrity. Understanding the microbial communities present in these
environments is crucial for identifying potential biohazards that could
compromise the safety of laboratory personnel and the integrity of
experimental outcomes (Perkins et al., 2019). Specifically, the presence
of pathogenic microorganisms can lead to contamination of samples,
skewing results and ultimately affecting the validity of research
findings. Furthermore, regular microflora assessments foster a culture
of safety and compliance within laboratories, guiding the implementation
of best practices in hygiene and containment protocols. By
systematically monitoring microbial diversity, laboratories can not only
mitigate risks associated with laboratory-acquired infections but also
enhance their reputational standing in the eyes of regulatory bodies and
the scientific community. Thus, microflora assessment emerges as a vital
tool in ensuring that research processes are both safe and credible,
reinforcing the ethical standards of scientific inquiry (Gebo & Lau,
2020).

2.9​ Strategies for Controlling and Preventing Microbial Contamination

2.9.1 Laboratory sterilization techniques (e.g., autoclaving, UV
radiation, chemical disinfectants)

Sterilization techniques are essential in laboratory settings to
eliminate microbial contamination and ensure the integrity of
experiments, cultures, and samples.

i\. Autoclaving, which uses pressurized steam at high temperatures
(typically 121°C at 15 psi for 15-30 minutes) to kill bacteria, viruses,
fungi, and spores. This method is widely used for sterilizing glassware,
surgical instruments, and biological waste (Dioguardi et al., 2020).

ii\. UV radiation, which involves exposing surfaces, air, and water to
ultraviolet light (mainly UV-C at 254 nm). This damages microbial DNA,
rendering them unable to replicate. While effective for surface and air
disinfection, UV radiation has limitations, such as reduced penetration
and inefficacy against biofilms or heavily contaminated materials
(Herraiz-Carboné et al., 2021).

iii\. Chemical disinfectants are another critical sterilization
approach, used especially for heat-sensitive equipment and surfaces.
Common disinfectants include alcohols (e.g., ethanol and isopropanol),
which denature proteins and dissolve lipid membranes, making them
effective against many microbes but ineffective against spores (Rutala &
Weber, 2019).

iv\. Aldehydes (such as formaldehyde and glutaraldehyde) offer
broad-spectrum sterilization, including sporicidal activity, but require
proper ventilation due to toxicity (Marshall et al., 2024).

v\. Hydrogen peroxide vapor and ethylene oxide gas are used for
sterilizing delicate instruments and medical devices, as they provide
deep penetration without high temperatures. Each method has its
advantages and limitations, and the selection depends on the nature of
the material, the type of microorganisms to be eliminated, and safety
considerations (O'Dwyer, 2019).

2.9.2 ​Good Laboratory Practices (GLP) for microbiological safety

Good Laboratory Practices (GLP) in microbiology are essential for
ensuring accuracy, reliability, and safety when handling microorganisms.

i\. Personal protective equipment (PPE), including lab coats, gloves,
safety goggles, and face masks, which protect researchers from
accidental exposure to hazardous microbes.

ii\. Proper hand hygiene and decontamination protocols for work
surfaces, instruments, and waste disposal are also critical to
minimizing contamination risks. Additionally, laboratories should
enforce

iii\. Strict aseptic techniques, such as working near a Bunsen burner
flame to maintain sterility, using sterile pipettes and loops, and
avoiding unnecessary movement to reduce airborne contamination.

iv\. Biosafety cabinets (BSCs) provide a controlled environment for
handling pathogenic microbes, preventing both laboratory contamination
and the release of harmful agents into the air.

v\. Proper sample handling, storage, and disposal. Microbial cultures
should be clearly labelled and stored in designated areas with
appropriate temperature controls to maintain viability while preventing
cross-contamination.

vi\. Regular equipment maintenance ensures that autoclaves, incubators,
and biosafety cabinets function optimally.

vii\. Training in biosafety levels (BSL-1 to BSL-4) to understand
containment measures for different risk groups of microorganisms.

viii\. Establishment of incident reporting systems to address spills,
exposure, or equipment malfunctions promptly. Effective GLP practices
not only protect researchers and the environment but also maintain the
integrity and reproducibility of microbiological research (Bunn, 2022).

2.9.3 Role of personal protective equipment (PPE) in contamination
control

Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) plays a crucial role in preventing
microbial contamination in laboratory, clinical, and industrial settings
by acting as a barrier between personnel and harmful microorganisms. PPE
includes lab coats, gloves, face masks, goggles, and face shields, all
designed to minimize direct contact with pathogens and contaminated
surfaces. Gloves, for example, prevent the transfer of microbes from
hands to sterile equipment or biological samples, reducing
cross-contamination. Face masks and respirators protect against
inhalation of airborne pathogens, while lab coats prevent microbial
attachment to clothing, reducing the risk of carrying contaminants
outside the controlled environment. Proper use of PPE, including regular
changing and disposal of single-use items, is critical in maintaining
contamination control and ensuring biosafety. Beyond personal
protection, PPE is essential for maintaining a sterile work environment
and protecting research integrity. In microbiological and pharmaceutical
laboratories, improper use of PPE can lead to contamination of cultures,
leading to inaccurate results and compromised experiments. In medical
and food processing industries, PPE helps prevent the spread of
infectious agents and ensures compliance with health and safety
regulations. However, PPE alone is not sufficient; it must be
complemented by good hygiene practices, proper training, and adherence
to decontamination protocols. Regular PPE inspections, correct donning
and doffing procedures, and appropriate disposal methods further enhance
contamination control, ensuring both worker safety and environmental
protection (Morris & Murray, 2021; Le et al., 2024).

2.10 Occupational Health Risks Associated with Laboratory Microflora

Working in microbiology laboratories exposes personnel to various
occupational health risks due to potential contact with pathogenic and
opportunistic microorganisms. Laboratory microflora, which includes
bacteria, viruses, fungi, and protozoa, can cause infections through
accidental exposure via inhalation, ingestion, skin contact, or
accidental inoculation (needle sticks, cuts, or splashes). Some of these
microorganisms are classified under different biosafety levels (BSL-1 to
BSL-4) based on their potential to cause disease, with BSL-3 and BSL-4
pathogens posing the highest risks. Workers handling pathogenic
microbes, especially drug-resistant strains such as Mycobacterium
tuberculosis or Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA), face
significant health threats if proper biosafety measures are not
followed. Respiratory infections are a major concern, particularly in
laboratories handling airborne pathogens like Mycobacterium
tuberculosis, Legionella pneumophila, or viral agents such as influenza
and coronaviruses. These microbes can become aerosolized during culture
handling, pipetting, or centrifugation, increasing the risk of
inhalation exposure. Similarly, fungal spores, such as those from
Aspergillus species, can cause opportunistic infections, particularly in
immunocompromised workers. Improper ventilation, inadequate use of
biosafety cabinets, and failure to wear appropriate respiratory PPE,
such as N95 masks, can heighten the likelihood of occupational
respiratory illnesses in laboratory personnel (White, 2020; Lingaas &
Kalager, 2020).

Gastrointestinal infection is another significant risk which can occur
through accidental ingestion of microbial contaminants. Poor hand
hygiene, consumption of food or drinks in the laboratory, or improper
handling of contaminated equipment can lead to infections caused by
enteric bacteria such as Salmonella, Shigella, or Escherichia coli.
Viruses such as Hepatitis A and Norovirus, which are present in
faecal-contaminated samples, can cause severe illness if ingested. To
mitigate these risks, laboratory workers must adhere to strict
handwashing protocols, avoid touching their face while working, and
properly decontaminate work surfaces after handling potentially
hazardous samples. dermal and systemic infections can result from direct
skin contact with pathogenic microorganisms or exposure to toxins they
produce. Certain bacteria, such as Pseudomonas aeruginosa and
Clostridium perfringens, can cause skin infections or even systemic
illness if they enter through cuts or abrasions. Some fungal species,
such as Candida or Trichophyton, can cause skin and nail infections.
Additionally, laboratory-acquired zoonotic infections, such as Brucella
or Coxiella burnetii (Q fever), pose risks to personnel working with
animal samples (Mathur et al., 2020; Rath, 2021).

2.11 ​Regulatory Guidelines and Compliance for Laboratory Microbial
Safety

Regulatory guidelines for laboratory microbial safety are essential for
ensuring the safe handling, storage, and disposal of microorganisms,
thereby protecting laboratory personnel, the environment, and public
health. These guidelines are established by various national and
international regulatory bodies, such as the World Health Organization
(WHO), the Centres for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), the
Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA), and the Biosafety
in Microbiological and Biomedical 9 9 (BMBL) guidelines. Compliance with
these regulations helps mitigate the risks of accidental exposure,
contamination, and the potential spread of infectious agents.
Laboratories are classified into biosafety levels (BSL-1 to BSL-4) based
on the risk associated with the microorganisms being handled, with BSL-4
being the highest level, dealing with dangerous pathogens like the Ebola
virus. Each level has specific containment requirements, including
facility design, safety equipment, and operational protocols. One
critical component of microbial safety regulations is biosafety training
and personnel competency. Regulatory bodies require laboratory staff to
undergo regular training on biosafety procedures, including proper use
of personal protective equipment (PPE), aseptic techniques,
decontamination methods, and emergency response protocols. Training
programs emphasize risk assessment, which involves identifying potential
hazards, assessing exposure risks, and implementing control measures.
Laboratories handling infectious agents must have biosafety manuals and
standard operating procedures (SOPs) that outline step-by-step safety
measures for different procedures. Compliance with training and
documentation ensures that all personnel are well-equipped to handle
microbes safely and effectively (WHO, 2019; Gebo & Lau, 2020).

Laboratory containment and waste management are also key aspects of
microbial safety compliance. Regulatory guidelines specify the proper
use of biological safety cabinets (BSCs), autoclaves, and air filtration
systems to prevent airborne contamination. Laboratories must implement
stringent waste disposal methods, such as autoclaving, chemical
disinfection, and incineration, to neutralize hazardous biological waste
before disposal. In addition, laboratories working with genetically
modified microorganisms (GMOs) must comply with guidelines set by
organizations such as the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) and the
National Institutes of Health (NIH) to prevent unintended environmental
release. Failure to adhere to proper containment and disposal measures
can result in contamination, regulatory violations, and potential legal
consequences. Regulatory agencies also enforce periodic inspections and
audits to ensure compliance with microbial safety standards.
Institutions must maintain accurate records of biosafety assessments,
incident reports, and training certifications to demonstrate adherence
to regulations. Non-compliance can result in penalties, loss of research
funding, or laboratory shutdowns (Mota et al., 2021; Graden et al.,
2021).

organism, that is, the test organism produces catalase while absence of
bubble would indicate a negative test organism.

​This would be performed as described by Freschi (2005) where a drop of
normal saline would be placed on a clean grease free slide followed by
two drops of blood plasma. The test organism would be smeared onto the
slide with a sterilized inoculating loop and left for 5 minutes to
observe macroscopic agglutinations. Presence of agglutinations would
indicate a positive test organism while absence of agglutination
indicated a negative result.

incubated at 37 for 48 hours. The change of medium color from green to
deep blue would indicate a positive result while absence of color change
indicates a negative result.

The surface of the medium would be inoculated with the test organism and

indicate a positive organism while no color change indicated a negative
test organism.

CHAPTER FIVE

DISCUSSION, CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATION

5.1 Discussion

The health of individual in a particular environment is determined
greatly by the quality of air individual breathes. Therefore, there is
need for the assessment of air quality of any location from time to time
especially where individuals spend most of their day. The present study
investigate the indoor air quality of Microbiology, Biochemistry,
Chemistry and Biotechnology laboratory of Nile University of Nigeria
Abuja. The research findings highlight variations in bacterial colony
counts across different laboratories and times of day at Nile University
of Nigeria, Abuja. These differences in microbial loads reflect the
dynamics of human activity, hygiene practices, and laboratory usage
patterns. Below is a discussion and interpretation of the findings,
supported with scholarly references: The microbiology laboratory
exhibited the highest bacterial load in the evening, possibly due to
continuous use throughout the day and accumulation of microbial aerosols
from activities such as culturing and handling of bacterial specimens.
The biochemistry and chemistry laboratories also showed increased
bacterial loads as the day progressed, suggesting that human traffic and
activities significantly contribute to microbial contamination. The
biotechnology laboratory showed a peak in the afternoon, which could
correspond to peak periods of practical laboratory sessions or
experiments. These findings are consistent with previous studies
indicating that indoor microbial concentrations tend to rise with
increased occupancy and activity levels (Kim et al., 2018).
Additionally, inadequate ventilation or ineffective disinfection
protocols may also contribute to these elevated counts. High bacterial
loads in academic laboratories, especially microbiology labs, can pose
health risks to students and staff, particularly those with compromised
immunity (Kassem et al., 2019). It emphasizes the need for stringent
sanitation protocols, scheduled fumigation, and efficient ventilation
systems to reduce microbial burden in laboratory environments (WHO,
2020).

The detection of diverse bacterial species in air samples from various
laboratories at Nile University in Abuja, Nigeria, points to a
significant level of microbial air contamination. The presence of both
pathogenic and environmental bacteria underscores potential public
health risks, especially in settings where sterile conditions are
essential. Several bacteria identified in the air samples are known
opportunistic pathogens, frequently implicated in healthcare-associated
infections. Staphylococcus sp. and Staphylococcus saprophyticus are
commonly found on human skin and mucous membranes. Staphylococcus
saprophyticus, though less virulent than Staphylococcus aureus, is a
known cause of urinary tract infections, particularly in young women
(Kuroda et al., 2005). The presence of Staphylococcus species in such
settings is not uncommon, as they are known to be part of the normal
human flora and can be transferred via contact with contaminated
surfaces or improper handling of laboratory materials (Lawal et al.,
2021; Ehlers & Merrill 2025).

The Gram-negative isolates included Klebsiella pneumoniae and Proteus
mirabilis, both of which are typically associated with hospital-acquired
infections and are indicative of poor hygiene or cross-contamination.
Klebsiella pneumoniae is a Gram-negative pathogen associated with
pneumonia, bloodstream infections, and urinary tract infections. It is
notorious for its role in hospital-acquired infections and antibiotic
resistance (Filev et al., 2025). Proteus mirabilis is frequently
isolated from urinary tract infections and is known for its strong
motility and urease activity, contributing to kidney stone formation
(Armbruster et al., 2018). This bacteria specie is often found in
decaying matter and fecal material, suggesting potential contamination
from organic substances.

Morganella morganii is a less common but clinically significant pathogen
that can cause sepsis, wound infections, and urinary tract infections,
particularly in immunocompromised individuals (Kakurai et al., 2025).
Enterococcus faecalis commonly found in the gastrointestinal tract, is
another opportunistic pathogen that exhibits resistance to several
antibiotics and is capable of causing endocarditis, bacteremia, and
wound infections (Ramoset al., 2020). These pathogens\' presence in
laboratory air samples may stem from inadequate air filtration, human
shedding (e.g., skin, respiratory droplets), or poor ventilation, all of
which can facilitate microbial dissemination in enclosed environments.
Other bacteria detected are commonly found in environmental reservoirs
but still pose contamination or health risks in laboratory settings: For
instance Micrococcus luteus is a ubiquitous Gram-positive bacterium
found in soil, water, and air. While generally non-pathogenic, it may
act as an opportunist in immunocompromised individuals and can
contaminate sterile products (Shi et al., 2023). Micrococcus luteus,
though generally harmless, is commonly found in dust and air, reflecting
its ubiquity in laboratory environments.

Arthrobacter luteus is typically soil-derived and rarely associated with
disease, but its presence indoors signals possible dust and air
contamination, especially in poorly maintained HVAC systems (Funke et
al., 1996; Scheublin & Leveau, 2013).Photobacterium sp. are marine
organisms rarely found in terrestrial air. Their detection may reflect
contaminated water sources or reagents used in the laboratories
(Urbanczyk et al., 2011; Fuertes-Perez et al., 2023). Thalassobacillus
sp., are halophilic bacteria, primarily found in saline environments.
Their presence in laboratory air suggests external contamination routes,
potentially through salt-containing media or reagents (Dutta &
Bandopadhyay, 2022). The occurrence of marine or halophilic bacteria
(Photobacterium, Thalassobacillus) in this study, highlights the
importance of strict laboratory hygiene protocols, especially after
regular working hours, to prevent microbial proliferation and
cross-contamination. The detection of Pseudomonas sp., in the
laboratories indicates the potential for contamination through water or
moist surfaces, as Pseudomonas species are known for thriving in moist
environments and are opportunistic pathogens (Diggle, & Whiteley, 2020;
Todar, 2020).

The presence of these bacterial species in laboratory air samples is an
indication of Inadequate ventilation and air quality control:as
laboratories must maintain high indoor air quality standards, including
HEPA filtration and regular air exchange, to limit microbial spread
(WHO, 2021). Pathogenic microbes in laboratory air may compromise both
the safety of personnel and the validity of experimental results,
particularly in microbiology, biotechnology, and cell culture
laboratories. Enhanced cleaning, routine environmental monitoring, and
controlled access can reduce microbial load in critical laboratory
environments (WHO, 2021).

5.2 Conclusion

The diversity of bacterial species detected in laboratory air samples at
Nile University indicates both human-associated and environmental
microbial contamination. The presence of opportunistic pathogens such as
Klebsiella pneumoniae, Staphylococcus spp., Proteus mirabilis, and
Enterococcus faecalis is particularly concerning due to their
association with healthcare-acquired infections. These findings
highlight the critical need for improved biosafety measures, enhanced
ventilation systems, and regular microbial air quality assessments to
mitigate infection risk and ensure a sterile working environment.

5.3 Recommendations

Based on the finding of this study, we recommend the following

i\. Regular cleaning and disinfection particularly after laboratory
sessions and strengthening hygiene protocols such as hand washing and
equipment sterilization.

ii\. Improved ventilation to reduce accumulation of airborne microbes or
implement HEPA filtration and UV sterilization where feasible to improve
air quality

iii\. Monitoring and Assessment: Routine microbial surveillance should
be conducted to track and control bioaerosol levels.
